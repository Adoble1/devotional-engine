# Poetic Flow Models

The poem agent should not be locked into a single hymn meter. It should choose a living structure that serves the chapter's emotional and theological movement.

## Classical Hymnody Craft

Public-domain metrical psalmody contributes structural craft, not a requirement to reproduce antique diction. The engine extracts these useful movements:

- Give each stanza a distinct dramatic task rather than paraphrasing the same claim repeatedly.
- Use biblical question and answer to build pressure and release.
- Return to a refrain only when repetition enlarges the scene.
- Carry theology through concrete verbs: ascend, stand, receive, lift, enter.
- Let syntax cross line endings and vary the placement of pauses.
- Treat stress and breath as primary; use exact meter only when the director explicitly selects it.
- Use rhyme for memory and resolution, never to justify filler.

Avoid automatic archaisms, identical stanza grammar, undeveloped refrains, and line-by-line prose explanation.

## Sonic Music

Euphony is a preference, not a substitute for truth. Favor rolling consonants such as `l`, `m`, `n`, `r`, `s`, `w`, and `h`, together with open vowels, when the chapter permits a flowing texture. Preserve harder clusters where battle, judgment, terror, fracture, or resistance need audible force.

Common meter may use an occasional first-foot trochaic inversion before returning to its prevailing iambic pulse. This should create emphasis, not replace one mechanical pattern with another. Because lexical stress cannot be reliably inferred from spelling alone, stress remains an agent/evaluator judgment rather than a deterministic hard check.

Use grammatical caesura in selected long lines. A comma, semicolon, colon, or dash should create a meaningful interior breath near the middle of the line. Punctuation added only to imitate music is another form of mechanism.

## Five Classic Flow Patterns

1. Blake, "The Tyger" - recursive interrogation
   - Starts with an unforgettable direct address.
   - Builds by repeated questions, pressure, and image recurrence.
   - Ends by returning to the opening question with a heightened verb.
   - Devotional use: when the chapter centers on awe, fear, mystery, or holy force.

2. Wordsworth, "The World Is Too Much With Us" - sonnet diagnosis and turn
   - Opens with a compressed complaint.
   - Lets the pressure gather in the octave.
   - Turns in the sestet toward desire, alternative vision, or lament.
   - Devotional use: when the reader needs diagnosis before invitation.

3. Keats, "Ode to a Nightingale" - lyric flight and return
   - Begins in bodily heaviness.
   - Rises through imaginative transport.
   - Passes through beauty, death, longing, and uncertainty.
   - Returns with a question rather than a closed explanation.
   - Devotional use: when the chapter moves through longing, transcendence, and chastened return.

4. Arnold, "Dover Beach" - scene to spiritual crisis
   - Begins with a calm external scene.
   - Lets sound and image destabilize the scene.
   - Expands the private perception into a cultural or spiritual condition.
   - Ends with intimate appeal under pressure.
   - Devotional use: when the chapter turns observation into exposed need.

5. Browning, "My Last Duchess" - dramatic disclosure
   - Uses a speaking persona instead of explanation.
   - Lets character, danger, and truth emerge indirectly.
   - Gains force through what the speaker does not understand about himself.
   - Devotional use: when the chapter exposes self-deception, power, envy, or misplaced trust.

## Engine Guidance

- Prefer organic form over mechanical compliance.
- Let repeated images carry the argument.
- Allow codas, uneven stanza lengths, dramatic address, sonnet-like turns, and recursive refrains.
- Keep deterministic safety checks for source grounding, Christology anchors, forbidden labels, repeated construction, and artifact integrity.
- Use strict common meter only when the director brief explicitly asks for `poem_form: common_meter`.

## Psalm 20 Application

"The Naked Trust" follows a dramatic-image structure:

- Stanza 1: trouble shakes the body and turns the eye toward the holy hill.
- Stanza 2: chariots and horses become the visible pressure of false trust.
- Stanza 3: the machinery collapses while the people stand.
- Stanza 4: Christ bears the crushing weight and the Spirit holds the believer.
- Coda: the governing image resolves into confession.
